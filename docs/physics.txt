Physics is the fundamental natural science that studies matter, energy, space, and time, seeking to understand the basic principles governing the universe. From the smallest subatomic particles to the largest cosmic structures, physics provides the mathematical framework and experimental methods to describe how the physical world operates. Classical physics, developed by pioneers like Newton and Maxwell, explains everyday phenomena such as motion, gravity, electricity, and magnetism. These principles remain essential for engineering applications, from building bridges to designing electrical circuits, and continue to accurately predict the behavior of objects at human scales and moderate speeds.

Modern physics emerged in the early 20th century with two revolutionary theories that transformed our understanding of reality. Quantum mechanics describes the bizarre behavior of matter and energy at atomic and subatomic scales, where particles can exist in multiple states simultaneously and exhibit wave-particle duality. Einstein's theory of relativity revealed that space and time are interconnected, that gravity results from the curvature of spacetime, and that the speed of light represents an absolute cosmic speed limit. Today, physicists continue pushing the boundaries of knowledge, exploring dark matter and dark energy that comprise most of the universe, searching for a unified theory that reconciles quantum mechanics with general relativity, and investigating exotic phenomena like black holes, gravitational waves, and the possibility of multiple dimensions beyond our perception.
